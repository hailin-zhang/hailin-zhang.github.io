Fatigue Science is a SaaS-enabled wearable that tracks a user's actigraphy, using the SAFTE algorithm developed by the United States Air Force over 20 years for over $40 million USD to predict the wearer's fatigue with a clinically proven 92% accuracy. Used by high performance athletes such as the Seattle Seahawks, the U.S. Military, as well as industry workers across the globe, our mission at Fatigue Science is to make fatigue and sleep a manageable process, increasing productivity and reducing fatigue-related accidents.

You might notice that our mobile team doesn't follow the usual development strategies for mobile applications - instead, we're developing a hybrid app using the Ionic framework! If you're not familiar, Ionic is a wrapper for Angular (and, more recently, many other web-based APIs) to access the native components of the mobile device. Because we're a relatively small and new startup, developing a hybrid application allows us to quickly deploy our code, as well as allowing us to easily manage our Android and iOS platforms simultaneously on a single code base. Hackathons

Lumohacks


Lumohacks was a 24-hour Health-based hackathon that I attended over at SFU this year. With 3 of my friends, we created Project Shift: A cross-platform service which enables patients suffering from substance addiction/abuse to prevent triggers which cause their condition, alerting help if necessary. A smart watch application will respond to physiological triggers (i.e. heart rate, respiration, etc.), and if a period of heightened stimulus is detected, signalling a trigger event, will vibrate for 5 seconds to ground the patient's thought stream and distract them. Then, it will open the mobile application, upon which the patient is required to take a photo of the trigger event and/or what they are doing, which will be uploaded to a cloud server that is linked to our web application. Monitored by volunteers and/or workers and family members, the photos will be validated manually, upon which an appropriate response is made: if approved, the trigger event will be marked as completed; otherwise, the patients' (previously inputted) contacts are notified (i.e. family, close friends, coworkers, etc.), prompting them for a call or a text, escalating to a call to emergency services if there is no response. After the campaign is over, we plan on adding machine learning components to our project, reducing the workload on manual labour by automatically detecting and verifying uploaded photos. Our hard work earned us a top 15 placement!
